Experience

Astrophysics Research (Dr. Cline)

Along with a collegue we studied the maximal amount of dark matter allowed in neutron stars given the current experimental data and standard equations of state. To do this we treated dark matter as a mirror copy of the neutron. Using the theory for degenerate Fermi gases we incorporated dark matter into the equations for neutron stars, and then used Runge-Kutta methods to get results. We were able to show that for a polytropic equation of state neutron stars cannot contain more than 16% dark matter.

Honors Math Research Project (Dr. Nave, Dr. Navarra)

"Finite Difference and Discrete Event Simulation Applied To Copper Smelter Dynamics"

The objective was precise quantitative modelling of a copper smelter. Such a model is important since it reduces the risk when investing large capital into smelter equipment. The main issue with previous models is accounting for the interplay of discrete events in the factory, with continuous dynamics inside the furnaces. This was fixed by incorporating the model into a discrete event simulation. To solve the continuous dynamics, I used Runge-Kutta methods on the thermochemical equations describing the furnace. These have no closed form solution, so at each iteration of the method Newton iterations would be done until convergence. To incorporate this into the discrete event simulation, thresholds like maximal temperatures and chemical concentrations where placed to trigger events. For example, during the smelting process slag forms and needs to be removed, this must be done when a certain concentration is reached. I modeled this concentration as a threshold which would trigger an event. To accommodate the desired precision, calculation of threshold crossing events needed to improve without increasing computational cost. I used Richardson extrapolation to solve this issue and showed that the errors in the estimation converge to zero. Part of my work was published in the proceedings of Philip Mackey Honorary Symposium along with the multiphysics simulations done by other collaborators. This solution would have been impossible without the joint expertise of mathematicians and engineers. It was my background in physics and mathematics that allowed me to act as a bridge in this collaboration, which really taught me the importance of multidisciplinary training.

Paper

The Ottawa Hospital Research Institute

"Adaptive margins with an early system for motion-tracking errors in liver SBRT"

Liver cancer radiation therapy is complex due to the deformability of abdominal organs, consequently real time prediction of errors during treatment is desired. So, I successfully developed a machine learning algorithm from the ground up that predicts errors with 84% accuracy. For this, I used techniques like support vector machines, clustering, time series analysis, and statistical features. I also developed a user-friendly interface for adaptive breathing control to be used by patients, to ease treatment. This research was published for the 61st American Association of Physicists in Medicine Annual Meeting an accomplishment I am particularly proud of.

"Exploring the use of Mutual Information as a Fitness Function for Parameter Reduction"

The aim of my project was understanding mutual information as a cost function for parameter reduction in an immune detection model. To determine the leading parameters in the dynamics I reduced the dimensionality of the system and analyzed its behavior. Since this relied heavily on the cost function it was important to find an optimal choice. So, I numerically analyzed the behavior of the eigenvalues in the system, which was represented by coupled differential equations, and showed that the mutual information performs better than other cost functions.
